## Summary

The entrainment hypothesis [Petitgas et al. 2006] posits that migratory routes in fish
populations are socially transmitted from experienced repeat spawners to inexperienced
conspecifics, producing contingent structure with weak genetic differentiation but strong
behavioural conservatism. Griffin et al. (2025, *Fish and Fisheries*) synthesised support
for entrainment in Atlantic tarpon (*Megalops atlanticus*) by testing five biological
hypotheses with a combination of literature review, life-history data, mark-and-recapture
records, and electronic tagging. The synthesis identifies three discrete Western Atlantic
contingents with limited but documented mixing, evidence for adult vagrancy as a
colonising mechanism, and the policy implication that managing across contingent
boundaries is required to maintain migratory knowledge.

## Key points

### What entrainment means

The entrainment hypothesis posits that migratory routes develop in fish populations
through interactions between experienced, repeat spawners and inexperienced conspecifics
(Petitgas et al. 2006). These social interactions transmit migratory knowledge and
collective memory, providing a mechanism for contingent structure that persists despite
weak genetic differentiation [Griffin et al. 2025, §Introduction, p. 734]. If collective
memory is lost, that loss may be a precursor to local-population or even
whole-population collapse.

### Three Western Atlantic Atlantic tarpon contingents

The three most well-understood Western Atlantic contingents are:

1. **Western Gulf of Mexico contingent** (centred on Texas / NW Gulf)
2. **Eastern Gulf of Mexico contingent** (centred on western Florida)
3. **Eastern US Seaboard contingent** (Atlantic coast of Florida and northwards)

The Western and Eastern GOM contingents overlap but divide near the Mississippi Delta in
Louisiana [Stephens et al. 2024]. The Eastern GOM and Eastern US Seaboard contingents
overlap in South Florida during spawning and overwintering months [Griffin et al. 2023].
Despite these mixing areas, "while contingent structures exist, there appears to be some
level of overlap in 'mixing areas'." [Griffin et al. 2025, §2.1, p. 736]

### Life-history scaffolding

- **Larval (leptocephalus) stage:** 20-40 days [Shenker et al. 2002].
- **Post-metamorphic juvenile habitat:** upper estuarine zones, mangrove swamps,
  marshes, ephemeral coastal ponds; air-gulping in anoxic/hypoxic water provides refuge
  from most predators and competitors [Seymour et al. 2008].
- **Juveniles remain in back-water habitat until** ~30 cm fork length (~1 year) or
  sometimes ~60 cm FL (~2 years) at ephemerally-connected sites.
- **Shift to coastal regions** at ~10.5 years and ~125 cm fork length [Crabtree et al.
  1997; Kurth et al. 2019].
- **Adult migratory cue:** 26°C isotherm [Luo et al. 2020].
- **Juvenile minimum lethal temperature:** mean 13.7°C [Mace et al. 2017].

### Evidence for behavioural conservatism

From a multi-year acoustic-telemetry dataset (Griffin et al. 2023), large juvenile tarpon
were generally resident or nomadic until nearing maturity, at which point they began to
adopt adult-like migratory behaviours with high repeatability (conservatism). Individuals
displayed high intra-individual variability and high inter-individual repeatability in
migratory strategies. Critically: "Among migrants, there was no relationship between
size and migratory behaviour." [Griffin et al. 2025, §2.1, p. 735] This pattern suggests
that movement strategies are not solely driven by environmental cues or swimming
capacity, but by social facilitation or learning, which is consistent with entrainment.

### Evidence for vagrancy

- **Adult vagrancy is documented:** tarpon recorded in cooler temperate waters off Nova
  Scotia, the Azores, and across northwest Europe including Ireland, France, Spain, and
  Portugal [Twomey and Byrne 1985; Bañón et al. 2019; Luo et al. 2020].
- **Acoustically-tagged tarpon have been detected** as far north as coastal Virginia,
  Maryland, and Delaware [Griffin et al. 2023].
- **Hypothesised vagrancy mechanism:** Gulf Stream features (eddies, altered currents)
  may interrupt entrained movements; juveniles at the older size class (> 30 cm FL) may
  also act as vagrants if they have not yet been entrained.

### Texas tarpon fishery collapse as a case study

Port Aransas, Texas, was the "Tarpon Capital of the World" in the 1920s, peaked in the
1930s, and the fishery had collapsed by the 1960s [Holt et al. 2005; Stilwell 2011]. The
collapse was linked to recruitment failure from degraded nursery habitats (freshwater
diversions, prey base depletion, water quality issues). Recovery has been multi-decadal:
juvenile abundances increased since the 1990s [Stephens et al. 2024], but the recovery
process in the Western GOM contingent took decades.

Globally, Atlantic tarpon landings declined by > 80% between 1965 and 2007 [Adams et al.
2014; Griffin et al. 2025, §2.2, p. 736]. Colombia provides a particularly stark case:
at a single port alone, the commercial fishery approached nearly 600 t annually in the
1960s before collapsing completely [Garcia and Solano 1995].

### Management implications

- The Atlantic tarpon stock is IUCN-listed as "vulnerable" [Adams et al. 2019].
- Contingent-level management is needed because collapse can be local (Texas / Western
  GOM) and recovery can be slow when entrainment knowledge has been lost.
- Coordinated cross-contingent management is required to preserve the migratory
  knowledge that supports population resilience.
- Research priorities: contingent structure, larval recruitment patterns, abundance
  trends.

## Five biological hypotheses tested (Griffin et al. 2025 framework)

| # | Hypothesis | Evidence type | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Is there spatial and temporal overlap between juveniles and adults? | Consistent | YES (life-history scaffolding) |
| 2 | Do all spawning units recover uniformly? | Consistent | NO (Western GOM slow recovery; Colombian Caribbean no recovery) |
| 3 | Which fish are the colonisers (juveniles vs adults)? | Consistent | Adult vagrancy more clearly documented; juvenile vagrancy possible |
| 4 | Do juveniles show behavioural differences in presence vs absence of adults? | Supportive | YES (suggests social learning) |
| 5 | Do adults overlap or cross contingents in mark-and-recapture? | Demonstrative | YES (some mixing, with maintained contingent identity) |

## Methods and approaches (Griffin et al. 2025 methodology)

- Literature reviews via Google Scholar with hypothesis-specific keyword sets
- Synthesis of life-history data from published studies
- Mark-and-recapture records (NOAA Cooperative Atlantic Tarpon Tagging Program)
- Electronic tagging (acoustic telemetry, cooperative networks)

The paper is itself a synthesis methodology that can be templated for other migratory
marine fish facing similar stock management challenges. The five-hypothesis
classification (consistent / supportive / demonstrative evidence) is the operational
framework.

## Open questions and research priorities

- **Genetic-vs-behavioural structure:** further work needed to disentangle how much
  contingent identity is genetic vs entrained (Petitgas et al. 2010 framework).
- **Mixing-area dynamics:** the spatial extent and frequency of cross-contingent mixing
  at the Mississippi Delta and South Florida areas is under-quantified.
- **Larval recruitment:** baseline recruitment-rate data are sparse across contingents,
  limiting recovery-trajectory inference.
- **Vagrancy frequency:** the per-individual rate of vagrancy in adults vs juveniles
  remains uncertain; better-characterised electronic-tagging coverage of contingent
  edges would help.
