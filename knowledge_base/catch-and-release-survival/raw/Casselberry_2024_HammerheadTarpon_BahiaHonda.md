# Source extraction: Casselberry et al. 2024, Marine and Coastal Fisheries — Hammerhead-Tarpon Depredation at Bahia Honda

## Verbatim abstract

> "Objective: Shark depredation, the full or partial consumption of a hooked fish by a
> shark before it is landed, is an increasing source of human-wildlife conflict in
> recreational fisheries. Reports of shark depredation in the catch-and-release Tarpon
> (also known as Atlantic Tarpon) Megalops atlanticus fishery in the Florida Keys are
> increasing, specifically in Bahia Honda, a recreational fishing hot spot and a putative
> Tarpon prespawning aggregation site.
>
> Methods: Using visual surveys of fishing in Bahia Honda, we quantified depredation
> rates and drivers of depredation. With acoustic telemetry, we simultaneously tracked 51
> Tarpon and 14 Great Hammerheads (also known as Great Hammerhead Sharks) Sphyrna
> mokarran, the most common shark to depredate Tarpon, to quantify residency and spatial
> overlap in Bahia Honda.
>
> Result: During the visual survey, 394 Tarpon were hooked. The combined observed shark
> depredation and immediate postrelease predation rate was 15.3% for Tarpon that were
> fought longer than 5 min. Survival analysis and decision trees showed that depredation
> risk was highest in the first 5–12 min of the fight and on the outgoing current. During
> the spawning season, Great Hammerheads shifted their space use in Bahia Honda to
> overlap with Tarpon core use areas. Great Hammerheads restricted their space use on the
> outgoing current when compared to the incoming current, which could drive increased
> shark-angler interactions.
>
> Conclusion: Bahia Honda has clear ecological importance for both Tarpon and Great
> Hammerheads as a prespawning aggregation and feeding ground. The observed depredation
> mortality and postrelease predation mortality raise conservation concerns for the
> fishery. Efforts to educate anglers to improve best practices, including reducing fight
> times and ending a fight prematurely when sharks are present, will be essential to
> increase Tarpon survival and reduce shark-angler conflict." (Abstract, p. 1)

### Site and study system

- **Site:** Bahia Honda Channel, easternmost border of lower Florida Keys, between
  Bahia Honda Key (east) and West Summerland Key (west) (§Methods, p. 3)
- **Channel depth:** ~7.6 m (Hopkins 1986; Office of National Marine Sanctuaries 2009)
  (§Methods, p. 3)
- **Mean current speed:** 0.11 m/s (Smith 1994) (§Methods, p. 3)
- **Bridges:** U.S. Route 1 Bridge (1982, in use) and Henry Flagler Overseas Railroad
  Bridge (1912, decommissioned); both ~2 km long (§Methods, p. 3)
- **Management context:** within Florida Keys National Marine Sanctuary (FKNMS)
  (§Methods, p. 3)

### Tarpon fishery context

- **Spawning aggregation timing:** April-June, SST 26°C-28°C (Griffin et al. 2022a)
  (§Intro, p. 2)
- **Majority of females complete spawning by July** (Crabtree et al. 1997) (§Intro, p. 2)
- **Florida flats fishery (tarpon + permit + bonefish) annual economic impact for south
  Florida:** USD 272 million (Smith et al. 2023) (§Intro, p. 2)
- **Florida tarpon fishery: almost exclusively catch and release; FWC sells limited
  tags; <44 tags sold per year since early 2000s** (Guindon 2011) (§Intro, p. 2)

### Study design

- **Visual surveys conducted from old railroad bridge overlooking U.S. Route 1 Bridge**
  (§Methods, p. 3)
- **Stratified random sampling design (5 days/week, including both weekend days and
  three weekdays) from April 1 (date range continues beyond page 3)** (§Methods, p. 3)
- **Anglers anchor between U.S. Route 1 bridge pilings; boat orientation depends on
  current direction (anchor north of bridge on outgoing/ebb; south on incoming/flood)**
  (§Methods, p. 3)
