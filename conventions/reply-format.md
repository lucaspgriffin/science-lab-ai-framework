# Reply format: Griffin Lab reviewer-reply conventions

This file is a **delta** on `conventions/voice.md` and `conventions/manuscript-format.md`. Use
this file when drafting a response-to-reviewers document or a proposal-resubmission narrative
that addresses prior panel comments.

For proposal-resubmission writing specifically, also load
`griffin-writing-style/references/reviewer-response.md` Part B.

---

## 1. Response document structure

```
# Response to Reviewers: [Manuscript ID]

[Opening paragraph, 2-3 sentences. Substantive, not boilerplate. Signal that you actually
engaged with the feedback.]

## Reviewer 1

### R1-C1
*[verbatim reviewer comment]*

[Response text, 2-5 sentences. Lead with what you did or why you held the line. Cite specific
manuscript locations or evidence.]

Manuscript changes: [section / paragraph / page or line refs in the revised manuscript]

### R1-C2
...

## Reviewer 2
...

## Editor comments
...

[Closing paragraph, 1-2 sentences. Confirm resubmission. No further reframing.]
```

---

## 2. Comment labelling

- **R[N]-C[M]**: Reviewer N, Comment M (e.g., R1-C3).
- Internal triage classification (not in the reply document itself):
  - **AGREE**: reviewer is right; manuscript updated to reflect the suggestion.
  - **PARTIAL**: reviewer raises a valid point; the full fix is out of scope; explain and
    partially accommodate.
  - **PUSHBACK**: reviewer's premise is incorrect, the suggestion would worsen the paper, or
    the comment misreads the manuscript. Push back politely with specific evidence.
  - **ADD-CITE**: reviewer wants a citation added; verify the citation supports the claim,
    then add.
  - **NEW-ANALYSIS**: reviewer requests a new analysis; budget the work, do it if feasible,
    defer with justification if not.
  - **RESTRUCTURE**: reviewer wants paragraphs or sections moved; consider the trade-off
    against the existing narrative.

---

## 3. Tone with reviewers

Professional, non-defensive, and genuinely engaged with criticism. The reader is a busy
editor or associate editor scanning to confirm major concerns have been addressed; they are
also judging whether the authors take feedback without resentment.

- **Never be dismissive.** Comments that feel wrong are usually pointing at something real,
  either a gap in explanation or an assumption that was not stated. Even when a reviewer has
  misunderstood the work, the problem is usually that the explanation was not clear enough
  for a smart, critical reader. Treat that as a writing problem, not a reviewer problem.
- **Never be sycophantic.** Do not say "the reviewer makes an excellent point" if what is
  meant is "the reviewer identified a problem I have now fixed." Be direct: "We have revised
  the methods section to clarify X."
- **Use first-person plural** ("we") throughout, matching manuscript voice.
- **Quote the reviewer's wording verbatim** when responding; avoid paraphrasing in a way that
  softens or hardens their point.

The tone should signal: *I read this carefully, I understand what you are asking, and I have
made a thoughtful decision about whether and how to address it.*

---

## 4. Opening line patterns

**Strong openers** acknowledge the substance of the review:
- "We appreciate the detailed comments on our statistical approach and have substantially
  revised the methods section in response."
- "The reviewer raises an important point about the ecological relevance of our experimental
  design, which we have now addressed by ..."
- "We thank the reviewers for pushing us to clarify the mechanisms underlying our results."

**Weak openers** (do not use):
- "We thank the reviewers for their careful reading of the manuscript." (Boilerplate; tells
  nothing about what was done.)
- "We are grateful for the opportunity to revise our work." (Formal but hollow.)
- "We have made numerous changes in response to reviewer comments." (Too vague.)

---

## 5. When to push back

Pushback is structurally important. A reply that accepts every comment without question
signals that the authors are anxious, not that the manuscript is good. Reviewers can be
wrong, and editors expect authors to defend the science.

Push back when:
- The reviewer's premise is factually incorrect (e.g., they request an analysis already in
  the supplement).
- The suggested change would weaken the paper (e.g., removing a control that the authors
  believe is load-bearing).
- The comment misreads the manuscript and the requested change would create internal
  inconsistency.

Pushback rules:
- Always cite specific manuscript locations or specific external evidence.
- Lead with the evidence, not the disagreement: "The cited analysis (Figure 3B, Methods
  p. 12) addresses this; we have added a sentence in the Results to make the connection
  clearer."
- Never push back on stylistic preferences (figure colour, paragraph order) unless the change
  would materially harm the paper.
- Push is two to three sentences maximum, followed by an offer of a compromise where one
  exists: "While we maintain that [position], we have added a sentence on page X clarifying
  [the reviewer's concern]."
- Never say "the reviewer is wrong." Use "We respectfully disagree" and provide evidence.

Worked example for an "I disagree with your interpretation" comment:

> Reviewer comment: *"The authors conclude that the observed decline in recruitment is driven
> by larval starvation, but these patterns are equally consistent with increased predation
> mortality. The paper presents no direct evidence for starvation."*
>
> We understand the reviewer's concern about causal attribution. The reviewer is correct that
> predation and starvation are not mutually exclusive, and we have revised the manuscript to
> reflect this. However, we maintain that starvation is the primary driver for the following
> reasons: (1) gut fullness assays on collected larvae (Figure 2, now discussed in detail on
> page 7) show significant depletion of egg reserves starting 5 days post-settlement,
> precisely when recruitment success declines; (2) laboratory feeding trials (Appendix C,
> new) demonstrate that larvae cannot meet their caloric requirements at the observed prey
> concentrations in our study region during the recruitment window; (3) predation rates
> measured with microsatellites do not correlate with recruitment success (Supplementary
> Table 1), whereas prey availability does (r = 0.78, p < 0.01, Figure 4). We have revised
> the Discussion to frame recruitment failure as a "trophic bottleneck" that may be
> exacerbated by, but is not solely attributable to, predation pressure.

## 6. Per-comment reply structure

Each individual reply has a predictable shape. Reviewers scan; they do not read the response
document the way they read the manuscript.

1. **Opening line**: one of the following classifications:
   - "We agree and have ..."
   - "The reviewer is correct; we have ..."
   - "We have addressed this by ..."
   - "We respectfully maintain that ..." (for pushback)
2. **Body**: 2–5 sentences. Cite evidence (figure refs, methods locations, external papers).
   For NEW-ANALYSIS comments, summarise the result with at least one quantitative number.
3. **Manuscript pointer**: explicit reference to where the change appears in the revised
   manuscript: "Manuscript changes: Results paragraph 3, page 7; new Supp Figure 8."

---

## 7. Contradictory reviewers

Acknowledge directly and explain the reasoning. "Reviewer 1 notes that the statistical
approach is overly complex; Reviewer 2 suggests the analysis needs additional rigor. We have
maintained our original approach because ..." This signals that the authors are not just
taking the last word they hear.

---

## 8. Major vs minor revisions

**Major revisions** carry more weight because the response is rebutting the entire panel's
assessment. Group related comments, show understanding of the scope of the criticism, and
explain the strategic response. If three reviewers worry about statistical power, that is not
three separate minor issues; that is a signal to overhaul how the analysis is presented.

For major revisions, consider a brief summary section at the top:

> Major revisions addressed:
> 1. Statistical power and multiple-comparisons correction;
> 2. Ecological interpretation of observed effect sizes;
> 3. Boundary conditions for the proposed mechanism.

Then address each group systematically.

**Minor revisions** have a lighter touch. Reviewers are not re-reading; brevity is fine.
Quote, respond, move on.

---

## 9. Tracked-changes manuscript conventions

The revised manuscript is submitted as a tracked-changes document. What gets tracked:
substantive changes in response to a reviewer comment (added sentences, modified claims, new
figures, expanded methods). What is silently edited: typo fixes, copy-edit cleanups
discovered during revision, and any change requested by no reviewer that improves clarity.
The tracked-changes document is paired with a clean-version document on submission.

---

## 10. Citations in the reply document

Reply document uses the same reference style as the manuscript (author-year alphabetical).
New references added in revision are inserted into the manuscript reference list; the reply
document uses the same author-year citation form.

When the reply cites a paper that is **not** being added to the manuscript (e.g., a paper
supporting a pushback but not load-bearing in the manuscript itself), the reply gives a full
inline citation (Author Year, journal) with the DOI.

---

## 11. Editor-comment handling

Editor comments get their own section at the end of the reply document. Tone is slightly more
formal than reviewer responses (editors set policy; reviewers offer opinions). When the
editor raises a substantive point already addressed by a reviewer response, cross-reference:
"We have addressed this in our response to Reviewer 2, Comment 3." When the editor flags
policy items (data deposition, code availability, ethics statements), respond with a
one-line confirmation and a manuscript pointer.

---

## 12. Closing

One closing paragraph after the editor comments:

> "We thank the reviewers and the editor for their detailed engagement with this work. We
> believe the revised manuscript is substantially stronger as a result, and we hope it is now
> suitable for publication in [journal]."

That is the entire closing; no apologies, no further reframing.
